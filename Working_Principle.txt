================================================================================
        INDUSTRIAL ROTARY SCREW AIR COMPRESSOR - WORKING PRINCIPLE
================================================================================

WHAT IS IT?
-----------
A rotary screw air compressor is an industrial machine that takes normal 
atmospheric air and compresses it to high pressure. This compressed air is 
then used to power pneumatic tools, automation systems, paint sprayers, 
manufacturing equipment, etc.

Unlike a piston compressor (which pumps up and down), a screw compressor 
uses two rotating helical screws (rotors) that continuously compress air 
without any pulsation. This makes it ideal for 24/7 industrial operation.


================================================================================
                         MAIN COMPONENTS
================================================================================

1. AIR INLET FILTER
   - Located at the entry point of the machine
   - Catches dust, dirt, and particles from atmospheric air
   - Protects the internal screw rotors from damage
   - Must be cleaned/replaced regularly

2. SUCTION VALVE (Load/Unload Valve)
   - Controls whether air enters the compressor or not
   - OPENS (Loading) when system pressure drops below minimum setpoint
   - CLOSES (Unloading) when system pressure reaches maximum setpoint
   - This is how the compressor regulates itself automatically

3. ELECTRIC MOTOR
   - The power source that drives everything
   - Typically 3-phase AC induction motor
   - Connected to the male rotor via coupling or belt drive
   - Rated in kW or HP (e.g., 7.5 kW, 15 kW, 37 kW, 75 kW, etc.)
   - Speed controlled by voltage/frequency (VFD in modern units)

4. SCREW COMPRESSION ELEMENT (The Heart of the Machine)
   - Contains TWO helical rotors inside a precision-machined chamber:
   
     MALE ROTOR (Drive Rotor):
     - Has 4 LOBES (convex profile)
     - Directly connected to the motor
     - Rotates and drives the female rotor
     
     FEMALE ROTOR (Driven Rotor):
     - Has 6 GROOVES (concave profile)
     - Meshes with the male rotor
     - Rotates in OPPOSITE direction
     - Ratio: When male does 1 full turn, female does 4/6 = 0.67 turns
   
   - The rotors never actually touch each other (oil film between them)
   - Air gets trapped between the lobes and chamber wall
   - As rotors turn, the trapped volume gets smaller and smaller
   - Smaller volume = Higher pressure (Boyle's Law)

5. OIL SEPARATOR VESSEL
   - Receives the compressed air + oil mixture from the screw element
   - Separates oil from compressed air using:
     a) Gravity (oil is heavier, drops to bottom)
     b) Coalescing filter element (catches fine oil mist)
   - Also serves as an oil reservoir
   - Contains the MINIMUM PRESSURE VALVE (MPV):
     Only opens when pressure exceeds ~3.5-4 bar
     Ensures enough pressure exists for oil circulation

6. OIL CIRCUIT (Return Loop)
   - Oil collects at the bottom of the separator vessel
   - Pressure difference pushes oil back to the compressor element
   - Oil passes through an OIL FILTER (removes contaminants)
   - Oil passes through an OIL COOLER (removes heat)
   - Clean, cool oil re-enters the compression chamber
   - Oil serves THREE purposes:
     a) SEALING - fills gaps between rotors for better compression
     b) COOLING - absorbs heat generated during compression
     c) LUBRICATING - reduces friction and wear on rotors/bearings

7. AFTERCOOLER
   - Cools the hot compressed air (can be 80-100°C after compression)
   - Either AIR-COOLED (fan blows ambient air over fins) or WATER-COOLED
   - Brings air temperature down close to ambient (35-45°C)
   - Causes water vapor to condense out of the air

8. WATER SEPARATOR / MOISTURE TRAP
   - Installed after the aftercooler
   - Collects liquid water that condensed during cooling
   - Drains water automatically or manually
   - Prevents water from entering the compressed air system

9. DISCHARGE OUTLET
   - Clean, cool, dry compressed air exits here
   - Connected to the plant's air distribution piping
   - Typical discharge pressure: 7-13 bar (100-190 PSI)


================================================================================
                    STEP-BY-STEP WORKING PROCESS
================================================================================

STEP 1: SUCTION
───────────────
  Atmospheric air (1 bar) → Inlet Filter → Suction Valve → Compression Chamber
  
  - Air is drawn in by the rotating screws creating a vacuum effect
  - Filter removes particles down to 5-10 microns
  - Suction valve opens fully when compressor is "loaded" (working)

STEP 2: COMPRESSION
───────────────────
  Air trapped between rotor lobes → Volume decreases → Pressure increases
  
  - Male rotor (4 lobes) meshes with Female rotor (6 grooves)
  - They rotate in opposite directions
  - Air enters at one end, gets trapped in pockets
  - As rotors turn, pockets move toward discharge end
  - Pocket volume shrinks progressively
  - Compression ratio typically 7:1 to 13:1
  - Oil is injected to seal, cool, and lubricate
  - Process is CONTINUOUS (no pulsation like piston compressors)

STEP 3: SEPARATION
──────────────────
  Air+Oil mixture → Separator Vessel → Oil drops out → Air continues
  
  - Mixture enters separator at high velocity
  - Centrifugal effect throws oil droplets to walls
  - Gravity pulls oil to bottom of vessel
  - Remaining oil mist passes through coalescing filter
  - Final oil content in air: less than 3 ppm (parts per million)
  - Oil returns to compressor through return line

STEP 4: COOLING
───────────────
  Hot compressed air (80-100°C) → Aftercooler → Cool air (35-45°C)
  
  - Fan-driven cooling fins extract heat
  - Temperature drops significantly
  - Water vapor condenses into liquid
  - Water is drained out

STEP 5: DELIVERY
────────────────
  Cool compressed air → Discharge valve → Plant air system
  
  - Air exits at set pressure (e.g., 8 bar / 116 PSI)
  - Ready for use in pneumatic tools, automation, etc.

================================================================================
              HOW THE 4 PARAMETERS INTERACT (PHYSICS)
================================================================================

The key insight: These parameters are NOT independent. They affect each other
in a coupled system, just like in real life:

    ┌──────────┐         ┌──────────────┐         ┌──────────────┐
    │ VOLTAGE  │────────>│ MOTOR TORQUE │────────>│  ROTATION    │
    │ (Input)  │         │ (Force)      │         │  (Speed)     │
    └──────────┘         └──────────────┘         └──────┬───────┘
                                                         │
                                                         │ Rotors spin
                                                         │ and compress air
                                                         ▼
    ┌──────────┐         ┌──────────────┐         ┌──────────────┐
    │VIBRATION │<────────│ MECHANICAL   │<────────│  PRESSURE    │
    │ (Output) │         │ STRESS       │         │  (Builds up) │
    └──────────┘         └──────────────┘         └──────┬───────┘
                                                         │
                                                         │ Creates back-
                                                         │ pressure (load)
                                                         ▼
                                                  ┌──────────────┐
                                                  │ RESISTS the  │
                                                  │ motor, slows │
                                                  │ rotation     │
                                                  └──────────────┘

INTERACTION RULES:
─────────────────
1. Voltage → Rotation:
   Higher voltage gives motor more torque, allowing faster rotation.
   If voltage is too low, motor cannot overcome the load and stalls.

2. Rotation → Pressure:
   Faster rotation compresses more air per second.
   Pressure builds up in the system over time.
   If rotation stops, pressure slowly leaks away.

3. Pressure → Rotation (FEEDBACK):
   Higher pressure creates more resistance (back-pressure).
   The motor has to work harder to maintain speed.
   This is why you need more voltage at higher pressures.

4. Rotation + Pressure → Vibration:
   Higher RPM = more centrifugal force = more vibration
   Pressure imbalance = pulsation = more vibration
   Worn bearings = more vibration at any speed
   Vibration is the KEY indicator of machine health.


================================================================================
              REAL-WORLD OPERATING CONDITIONS
================================================================================

NORMAL OPERATION:
  Voltage:    380-415V (3-phase) or 170-220V (single phase in simulation)
  Rotation:   1000-1500 RPM (direct drive) or 3000+ RPM (belt drive)
  Pressure:   7-8 bar (100-116 PSI) for general industrial use
  Vibration:  < 4.5 mm/s (ISO 10816 standard for "Good" condition)

WARNING CONDITIONS:
  Voltage:    > 250V or < 150V (power quality issue)
  Rotation:   Fluctuating or dropping (mechanical problem)
  Pressure:   Not reaching setpoint (leak, valve, or rotor wear)
  Vibration:  4.5-11.2 mm/s (needs attention soon)

DANGER / SHUTDOWN CONDITIONS:
  Voltage:    > 280V (insulation breakdown risk)
  Rotation:   < 100 RPM (stall - motor overheating)
  Pressure:   > 13 bar (safety valve should open)
  Vibration:  > 11.2 mm/s (bearing failure imminent)
  Temperature: > 110°C (thermal shutdown)


================================================================================
              WHY THIS SIMULATION MATTERS
================================================================================

1. PREDICTIVE MAINTENANCE:
   By monitoring these 4 parameters over time, you can predict when a 
   machine will fail BEFORE it actually breaks down. This saves:
   - Unplanned downtime (very expensive in factories)
   - Catastrophic damage (a seized rotor costs thousands to replace)
   - Safety incidents (pressure vessel rupture, electrical fire)

2. TRAINING:
   Operators can learn how the machine behaves under different conditions
   without risking a real $50,000-$200,000 compressor.

3. WHAT-IF SCENARIOS:
   - "What happens if voltage drops during peak load?"
   - "What happens if a bearing starts to wear?"
   - "What happens if we run at maximum pressure for too long?"
   - "How does temperature build up over extended operation?"

4. MACHINE LEARNING:
   The simulation generates realistic sensor data that can be used to 
   train ML models for anomaly detection and remaining useful life (RUL)
   prediction — which is exactly what your project does!

              HOW TO USE THE SIMULATOR
================================================================================

1. Open browser: http://localhost:7000
2. You'll see the process flow diagram of the compressor
3. Use the LEFT PANEL sliders to adjust parameters
4. Watch the SCHEMATIC respond:
   - Rotors spin faster/slower based on RPM
   - Air flow animation shows pressure
   - Oil circuit flows when machine is running
   - Temperature and vibration displays update
   - Machine shakes when vibration is high
5. The TELEMETRY PANEL (bottom-right) shows real sensor readings

TRY THESE EXPERIMENTS:
  
  Experiment 1: Normal Startup
    Set: Voltage=170V, Rotation=450 RPM, Pressure=100 bar, Vibration=40 mm/s
    Observe: Stable operation, everything in green
  
  Experiment 2: Increase Load
    Slowly increase Pressure to 200 bar
    Observe: Motor works harder, temperature rises, vibration increases
  
  Experiment 3: Overspeed
    Set Rotation to 2500 RPM
    Observe: High vibration, machine shakes, temperature spikes
  
  Experiment 4: Power Loss
    Drop Voltage to 100V while pressure is high
    Observe: Motor struggles, RPM drops, possible stall condition
  
  Experiment 5: Bearing Wear (High Vibration)
    Set Vibration to 100 mm/s
    Observe: Machine shakes violently, indicates bearing failure
